# Malta Spa Commercial & Operational Management Portfolio
## What I Would Do in the First 30–60–90 Days as Spa Manager

### Purpose
This plan is designed to show immediate managerial readiness in a Malta spa operating context.  
It translates the scenario network, outlet identity layer, and executive walkthrough into a practical first 90-day management approach.

The objective is not to redesign the business from day one.  
The objective is to establish control quickly, improve outlet-level decision discipline, stabilize service delivery, tighten commercial execution, and identify where growth, leakage, seasonality, and workforce pressure require different management responses.

### Management Principles
- Do not push revenue before service consistency is under control.
- Do not scale premium mix in strain-risk outlets before staffing stability improves.
- Do not treat seasonal demand as business success unless conversion quality holds.

---

## First 30 Days — Diagnose, Stabilize, Build Operating Trust

### Core objective
In the first 30 days, my priority is to establish outlet-level control fast: validate real operating conditions, identify execution leakage, stabilize service risk, and separate outlets that are ready to scale from outlets that require correction first.

### What I would focus on
- Validate the real operating rhythm of each outlet, not only the reported numbers
- Observe staffing pressure, shift discipline, and service flow in peak and non-peak periods
- Identify where demand is converting into clean value and where execution leakage is suppressing commercial quality
- Establish leadership credibility with therapists, outlet leads, and cross-functional partners through visible operating presence
- Review complaints, cancellations, no-shows, utilization quality, and pressure points that can weaken guest trust
- Separate outlets that are ready for controlled growth from outlets that require correction or stabilization first

### What I would do
- Conduct outlet walk-throughs across the seven-outlet network to validate actual floor conditions
- Review roster discipline, staffing pressure, and service consistency patterns
- Hold structured 1:1 conversations with key outlet leaders and team members to surface real execution friction
- Audit complaint themes, recovery cases, and peak-time service breakdowns
- Review commercial execution quality: package mix, premium mix, attach behavior, conversion quality, and revenue capture logic
- Flag immediate control failures that should be corrected now and avoid touching areas that would create unnecessary operating instability
- Establish a weekly management cadence for review, escalation, and corrective follow-up

### Expected outcome by day 30
- Outlet-by-outlet management reading validated against real operating conditions
- Leadership credibility established through visible floor engagement
- Top execution leakage points identified, assigned, and under active corrective follow-up
- High-risk service and staffing issues surfaced before growth is pushed
- Practical control baseline established for the next intervention phase

---

## Days 31–60 — Tighten Control, Correct Leakage, Improve Team Execution

### Core objective
Move from diagnosis into disciplined intervention.  
By this stage, the priority is to correct visible execution leakage, strengthen commercial discipline, reduce preventable strain, and improve workload control without creating organizational shock.

### What I would focus on
- Correct the most visible value leakage in high-demand outlets
- Reduce strain in pressure-sensitive outlets before pushing harder on sales
- Improve roster discipline, service consistency, and operating follow-through
- Protect guest experience while increasing commercial quality
- Strengthen local outlet accountability through simple ownership and measurable follow-up

### What I would do
- Prioritize St Julian's / Paceville Spa as a dual-risk case: improve value capture while controlling team pressure
- Prioritize Qawra / St Paul's Bay Spa for workforce stabilization, roster discipline, and service-risk reduction
- Improve conversion discipline in Mellieha Spa around seasonal demand windows
- Protect execution quality in growth-ready outlets before pushing harder on revenue
- Introduce action tracking for complaints, staffing issues, service recovery, execution leakage, and missed follow-through
- Coach outlet leaders on practical commercial-operational decision-making under pressure
- Tighten upselling, package strategy, premium mix discipline, and yield capture where guest trust can still be protected
- Escalate peak-time failures, service inconsistency, and staffing misalignment faster and more decisively

### Expected outcome by day 60
- Key execution leakage points reduced and under disciplined corrective control
- Top leakage cases under active corrective review
- Better roster control in strain-risk environments
- Stronger outlet accountability with named ownership by outlet
- Weekly escalation rhythm active for staffing, service, and execution issues
- More stable service delivery during higher-pressure periods
- Clearer management confidence on where growth can be pushed safely and where stabilization must continue

---

## Days 61–90 — Scale What Works, Formalize Management Discipline, Set Medium-Term Direction

### Core objective
Turn early control into repeatable management discipline.  
By this stage, the business should move from reactive correction into structured performance management with clearer commercial intent and stronger operating control.

### What I would focus on
- Convert early fixes into operating standards
- Formalize outlet review logic by outlet identity
- Push growth more confidently only in outlets that have earned the right to scale
- Continue protecting team sustainability and service quality
- Build a medium-term action roadmap that aligns commercial and operational priorities

### What I would do
- Formalize differentiated outlet playbooks by identity:
  - growth-ready
  - seasonal conversion play
  - team-strain / burnout-risk
  - leakage-risk
- Set clearer monthly review logic for commercial performance, service quality, staffing health, and corrective execution follow-up
- Scale premium mix, conversion quality, and yield capture only in growth-ready outlets with acceptable operating control
- Maintain tighter control over dual-risk and pressure outlets before aggressive expansion
- Define medium-term priorities for training, staffing discipline, guest recovery, revenue quality, and leadership accountability
- Build a management dashboard rhythm that supports decisions, not passive reporting
- Align outlet action plans with business priorities, team realities, and service constraints

### Expected outcome by day 90
- A more stable and repeatable management system across the network
- Clear distinction between growth outlets, correction outlets, and stabilization outlets
- Stronger commercial-operational integration at outlet level
- Weekly escalation rhythm active and used in management review
- Outlet playbooks adopted for management use
- Higher confidence in where the business can scale safely and where tighter control must remain
- A credible Spa Manager operating plan for the next phase of execution

---

## Closing Positioning
This 30–60–90 day plan is intentionally built as an execution plan for a Spa Manager in a multi-outlet environment.  
It is designed to show practical readiness: how to enter a new market, understand real operating conditions quickly, stabilize pressure, protect service quality, and translate performance signals into outlet-level action.
