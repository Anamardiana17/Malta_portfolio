### C. 3-Minute Interview Walkthrough
This project is built as a Malta Spa Commercial and Operational Management Portfolio for a seven-outlet network over a multi-year horizon from 2017 to 2025.  
I designed it to show how a Spa Manager can interpret outlet conditions under different market regimes, including baseline growth, shock, recovery, normalization, and stronger market demand.

The central idea is that not all outlets should be managed the same way.  
Some outlets are growth-ready and should be scaled with disciplined execution.  
Some are seasonal conversion plays, where the real challenge is turning demand windows into higher-quality revenue.  
Some are under operational pressure, where workforce stabilization needs to come before stronger commercial push.  
And one outlet, St Julian's / Paceville Spa, is intentionally framed as a dual-risk case where commercial leakage and team pressure coexist.

What makes the portfolio valuable is that each outlet reading leads directly to management priorities.  
For every outlet, I defined the executive reading, managerial focus, commercial priority, operational priority, and why that outlet matters from a management perspective.  
So this is not only a dashboard or classification project.  
It is a practical framework for deciding where to scale, where to stabilize, and where to protect value capture and service quality.

That is why I position it as a Spa Manager commercial and operational portfolio supported by analytics.  
The analytics provide the structure, but the real point is managerial judgment.

### K. Interview Answer — What Would You Do in Your First 90 Days?
In the first 30 days, my priority would be to establish outlet-level control fast.  
I would validate real operating conditions, identify execution leakage, stabilize visible service risk, and separate outlets that are ready to scale from outlets that require correction first.

From days 31 to 60, I would move into disciplined intervention.  
That means correcting visible leakage, tightening commercial execution, stabilizing pressure-sensitive outlets, and making sure accountability is active at outlet level rather than staying abstract.

From days 61 to 90, I would formalize what works.  
I would set clearer management cadence, build outlet playbooks by identity, and push growth only in outlets where operating control is strong enough to support it.

My management principle is simple: do not push revenue before service consistency is under control, do not scale premium mix in strain-risk outlets before staffing stability improves, and do not treat seasonal demand as business success unless conversion quality holds.

### L. Shorter Interview Answer — First 90 Days
My first 90 days would focus on three things: establish control fast, correct what is leaking value, and scale only what is operationally ready.  
I would first validate real outlet conditions, then stabilize service and staffing risk, and finally formalize outlet-specific management discipline so growth is pushed in the right places, not everywhere at once.
